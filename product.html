Product & business model

What we offer

VitalBee is an integrated aerial sensing platform for Search & Rescue: a rugged multi-rotor system with modular payloads (RF, acoustic, optional thermal), onboard edge computing, and a ground dashboard built for field decisions—not raw feeds alone.

Value for rescue teams

  • Faster, wider survivor localization after earthquakes, collapses, and similar disasters.
  • A probability heatmap with ranked priority points and short evidence summaries—reducing operator overload versus disconnected raw streams.
  • Two-stage workflow: wide-area adaptive grid scanning, then closer verification—aligned with how teams actually deploy.
  • Modular architecture so payloads and models can evolve without rebuilding core mapping and scoring software.

Commercial model

We use a hybrid model: one-time hardware (drone kit), annual software subscriptions, and optional maintenance and training. Institutional buyers typically procure through public-sector contracts; we grow through R&D programs, agency relationships, and pilot deployments.

First-month economics (planning)

The coursework exercise assumes one kit plus one annual subscription in the first month → $18,000 revenue ($15,000 + $3,000). Variable cost for that single drone is estimated at $12,500 (hardware, sensors, assembly & test), with roughly $10,000 more for salaries and software infrastructure—so the first month is not expected to be profitable, which is typical while R&D and unit costs dominate. The model expects improving margins as subscriptions scale and hardware is purchased in bulk.

Universities and labs support validation; public institutions enter through pilots and co-funded trials. Partner assumptions (access, affordability, willingness to work with a student-led venture) are explicit risks we monitor.

Customer relationship

Buyers are institutional—government SAR units and public agencies. The offering is mission-critical, so we plan for onboarding, training, technical support, maintenance, and continuous software updates to earn long-term trust.

Channels & costs

Reach is primarily through pilot projects, demonstrations, and procurement channels agencies already use for drones. Fixed costs include personnel, engineering tools, and core infrastructure. Variable costs include drone procurement or production, payload integration, edge compute, field testing, certification, and scaling operations—planned totals on the order of $25k–$50k next month, $40k–$90k next quarter, and $80k–$180k next year depending on prototype and certification pace.

Risks & strengthening the model

Competition from established SAR technology vendors, slow public procurement, and the need for extensive field validation before adoption are central risks. To reduce them, we can emphasize recurring software and services, explore leasing or drone-as-a-service to lower upfront capital for buyers, and tier subscriptions with advanced analytics and reporting to improve predictable revenue.
